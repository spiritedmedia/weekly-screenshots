How three other bakeries — and a Colorado commissioner’s comments — helped decide the Masterpiece case

Andrew Kenney Follow
Posted on June 4, 20183:41 pm Categories Denver news

Colorado’s legal process treated a Christian baker unfairly, according to the U.S. Supreme Court.

Colorado’s legal process treated a Christian baker unfairly, according to the U.S. Supreme Court.

That’s what a majority of the justices ruled today in the case of Jack Phillips, who refused to bake a wedding cake for Charlie Craig and David Mullins, a same-sex couple, in 2012.

The new ruling is “narrow” in its scope. It does not mean that religious business owners are now free to deny services to gay people. And a similar case could turn out very differently next time, analysts said.

But the justices took aim at some of the Colorado officials who had previously handled the case, and they set the stage for many more legal fights to come.

U.S. Supreme Court sides with Colorado baker on same-sex wedding cake, but big issue remains unresolved

Associated Press
Posted on June 4, 20189:15 am Categories Denver news

The court is not deciding the big issue in the case, whether a business can invoke religious objections to refuse service to gay and lesbian people.

By Mark Sherman, Associated Press

WASHINGTON — The Supreme Court ruled narrowly Monday for a Colorado baker who wouldn’t make a wedding cake for a same-sex couple. But the court is not deciding the big issue in the case, whether a business can invoke religious objections to refuse service to gay and lesbian people.

The justices’ limited ruling turned on what the court described as anti-religious bias on the Colorado Civil Rights Commission when it ruled against baker Jack Phillips. The justices voted 7-2 that the commission violated Phillips’ rights under the First Amendment.
